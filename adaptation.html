Disaster Resilience & Adaptation

Building Resilient Communities through Integrated Urban Planning and Climate Risk Modeling

Catastrophic natural disasters pose formidable challenges to the creation of resilient urban environments. While the direct impacts of hazards such as floods, hurricanes, and landslides are well-documented, the indirect and cascading consequences—which propagate through socio-ecological and infrastructure systems—are often underestimated. These cascading risks arise from interdependencies between natural and built environments, amplifying disaster impacts through infrastructure failure, population displacement, economic disruption, and social inequities.

My research aims to bridge this critical gap by examining how cascading effects spread across social, environmental, and physical systems in urban settings, especially under the combined pressures of climate change, land use transitions, and demographic shifts.

Quantifying Systemic Multi-Hazard Risks

I employ agent-based modeling (ABM), computational techniques, and geospatial analysis to quantify systemic risks posed by compound hazards—such as the interaction of storm surges and urban flooding. By integrating open data, hazard projections, and spatial geoinformation tools, I develop scalable frameworks for understanding, simulating, and visualizing how extreme climate events affect various communities and urban functions.

Human-Centered Adaptation Strategies

Beyond risk assessment, my research prioritizes urban planning and design approaches to reduce vulnerability and enhance resilience. With rapid urban expansion and growing populations, effective adaptation and mitigation strategies are urgently needed. Leveraging machine learning, artificial intelligence (AI), and scenario analysis, I assess interventions such as infrastructure retrofitting, managed retreat, and land use planning policies.

By connecting climate science with spatial planning and community design, my work delivers actionable insights—empowering planners, policymakers, and local stakeholders to co-develop resilient urban systems capable of withstanding future climate uncertainties.
