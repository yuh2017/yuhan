Data-Driven Risk Assessment

Relying on data from multiple sources, I apply data-driven techniques to examine future trends in urban development under climate change. These insights are integrated into an Agent-Based Modeling (ABM) framework that simulates human behavior during climate-induced disruptions. I explore innovative methodologies that leverage artificial intelligence (AI) to synthesize synthetic populations and generate realistic activity patterns. By modeling individual and household responses to compound and cascading hazards, my research enhances assessments of dynamic exposure and vulnerability, helping planners design human-centric, equitable adaptation strategies.

The outcomes of this research are visualized through digital twins—interactive 3D simulations that integrate environmental, social, and infrastructural data. These platforms provide planners, policymakers, and communities with intuitive tools for scenario exploration, facilitating informed decision-making and effective climate risk communication.

In parallel, I employ state-of-the-art methods from computer science, spatial statistics, and urban planning theories to address critical environmental challenges and promote social resilience. This work harnesses large-scale urban data—such as satellite imagery, open geospatial datasets, and sensor networks—to develop new analytical tools and geospatial models capable of identifying patterns of inequality, vulnerability, and resource access. Through these methods, I seek to improve how cities collect, process, visualize and use urban data to inform planning decisions.

Ultimately, my research contributes to the design of more sustainable, inclusive, and adaptable cities by providing scalable tools and strategies that support climate-resilient urban planning. By focusing on human behavior, environmental stressors, and spatial dynamics, this work aims to foster cities that not only withstand climate impacts but thrive through innovation and equitable planning practices.
